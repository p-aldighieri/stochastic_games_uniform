Feasibility is ranked against the packet’s exact target: for every (\varepsilon>0), one behavioral profile (\sigma) must be an (\varepsilon)-equilibrium for every sufficiently large finite horizon (T), uniformly over all initial states and unilateral deviations, not just payoff-form existence.  

1. **Hybrid modified-game + continuation-orbit construction.**
   **Core technique:** Use a statewise auxiliary modified game to cap continuation payoffs at minmax-compatible levels, then build a compact continuation region (W) and a map (f) whose nodes are finite equilibrium blocks. The modified-game paper already supplies the auxiliary-game architecture and the stationary-equilibrium side, while the 2025 non-rectangular absorbing proof shows how each continuation vector (w) can generate a finite-horizon (\varepsilon)-equilibrium block (\sigma(w)) with positive absorption hazard, after which an approximate finite orbit of (f) is concatenated into one global strategy. 
   **Key intermediate steps:** First, define partitions and cutoffs (c_i(D)) just above the uniform minmax values and prove a local block lemma in the full game: for every (w\in W), there is a block that is already an (\eta)-equilibrium of a (T(w))-stage game with terminal bonus (w), has controlled exit probability (\mu(w)>0), and yields a next continuation close to (f(w)). Second, prove an approximate finite-orbit theorem for the full continuation map, not just in the positive recursive absorbing subclass. Third, concatenate the blocks and attach public deviator-identification plus minmax punishments so the continuation vector forms an almost-supermartingale until absorption or detection. Fourth, turn that supermartingale control into finite-horizon average-payoff control, with the last partial block and all monitoring costs swallowed into the (\varepsilon) budget.
   **Likely failure point:** The hard seam is the local block lemma outside absorbing-like geometry. In the general game, punishments change transition structure, several recurrent classes may compete at once, and the continuation map may stop being tame enough to admit a finite-orbit argument. The other danger is that the route may deliver payoff-form invariance without yet yielding one uncorrelated profile that works for all large (T).
   **Complexity estimate:** Extreme, but modular. It is the most promising full-theorem route because the literature already has one engine for auxiliary payoffs and another for orbit concatenation; the missing bridge is brutal, but it is visible.

2. **Martin-function bootstrap to a global Nash selector.**
   **Core technique:** Avoid discounted asymptotics entirely. Work directly on public histories using the Martin function to define a continuation correspondence of minmax-safe promises, then try to select at each history a mixed action and next promise that satisfy a one-step incentive inequality. Existing Martin-function machinery already gives subgame (\varepsilon)-maxmin strategies, minmax (\varepsilon)-acceptable profiles, extensive-form correlated (\varepsilon)-equilibria, and an (\varepsilon)-solvable subgame, while explicitly relying on a deviation-detection ingredient for nonstationary play. 
   **Key intermediate steps:** Define a correspondence (W(h)) of continuation payoffs attainable from each public history (h) while keeping everyone above minmax. Prove a one-step self-generation property: for each (w\in W(h)), there is an uncorrelated mixed action profile (x(h)) and a continuation assignment (h'\mapsto w(h')\in W(h')) such that current promise is bounded by current payoff plus expected next promise plus a tiny leakage (\eta(h)). Then choose a measurable public-history selector and use public monitoring to identify a deviator and switch to a history-indexed punishment plan. Finally, telescope the promise inequalities over (T) stages so all leakage sums to at most (\varepsilon), uniformly in the initial state.
   **Likely failure point:** This route is allergic to missing convexity. The known Martin-function results produce individually rational profiles, correlated equilibria, or equilibrium in some subgame, but not yet a single uncorrelated profile across all histories and states. The one-step self-generation property needed here may simply be another mask worn by the open problem.
   **Complexity estimate:** Extreme to brutal. Conceptually cleaner than Route 1, but it demands a genuinely new selector theorem on the history tree.

3. **[SCOPE] Minimal-counterexample reduction to a rigid core.**
   **Core technique:** Treat the conjecture as a classification problem. Prove that any minimal counterexample must have a sharply constrained structure: no easy state, no (\varepsilon)-solvable subgame that can be spliced globally, no component governed by bounded-crossing maxmin partitions, and no non-rectangular positive recursive absorbing reduction. The needed ingredients already exist separately: (\varepsilon)-solvable subgames, Property (P) for partitions by uniform maxmin values, and the 2025 theorem eliminating non-rectangular positive recursive absorbing cores. The packet also flags that payoff-form and the stronger strategy-form target coincide only locally, not globally, so the reduction has to preserve the packet’s exact quantifiers.   
   **Key intermediate steps:** Choose a lexicographically minimal counterexample by number of states, then actions, then players. Show it cannot contain an easy initial state or a subgame that is already (\varepsilon)-solvable in a way that can be promoted to all states. Next, use the modified-game partitions (D_i^*) to peel off regions where the number of switches between continuation-value classes is uniformly bounded, and collapse closed or almost-absorbing regions into terminal payoffs while preserving any obstruction to uniform equilibrium. The hoped-for endpoint is a core that is strongly connected, strategically irreducible, and whose nonabsorbing geometry is forced to be rectangular in a very narrow sense.
   **Likely failure point:** Preservation lemmas are the minefield. It is much easier to preserve payoff nonexistence than to preserve the stronger “one profile works for every large (T) and every initial state” obstruction. A sloppy reduction can solve the wrong problem.
   **Complexity estimate:** Very high, but this is the best route for rigorous partial progress. Even a theorem saying “any counterexample must have structure X” would materially tighten the search space.

4. **Counterexample via horizon-tuning obstruction in a multiplayer quitting/absorbing gadget.**
   **Core technique:** Build a gadget where deterring one deviation requires monitoring or mixing at a density tuned to the horizon, while deterring another deviation requires a conflicting density. Then any fixed strategy profile suffers regret spikes along an infinite subsequence (T_k). The literature already throws sparks in that direction: every three-player absorbing game has an undiscounted equilibrium payoff, absorbing team games can force cyclic (\varepsilon)-equilibria, a classic three-player example has only cyclic Markov equilibria and no stationary (\varepsilon)-equilibria, and a four-player quitting game has a simplest equilibrium of period two. ([INFORMS Online][1])
   **Key intermediate steps:** Start from a four-player quitting or absorbing scaffold, since three-player absorbing games are already positive. Implant two competing “alarm clocks”: deviation A is profitable unless monitoring is frequent, while deviation B is profitable if monitoring is too frequent because the induced costs or exposure patterns change. Prove a dichotomy lemma saying that for any profile (\sigma), either the long-run density of monitoring blocks dips below a threshold infinitely often, enabling A, or exceeds a threshold infinitely often, enabling B. Then convert that dichotomy into a lower bound on best-response regret for infinitely many horizons.
   **Likely failure point:** Universal quantification over all behavioral strategies is the dragon here. Many tempting gadgets are defeated by elaborate nonstationary rescue plans, and proving that no such rescue exists is much harder than producing examples with only periodic or nonstationary equilibria.
   **Complexity estimate:** Brutal. The payoff for success is enormous, but as a first move it is less feasible than Routes 1 and 3.
